A final farewell to Rama IX

King Bhumibol Adulyadej, who died in October last year at the age of 88, was Thailand’s monarch for seven decades. Preparations for a lavish, five-day funeral with a budget of $90 million took nearly a year to complete, with thousands of artisans working on a funeral pyre set within a cremation complex of gold pavilions.

Royal Crematorium structure

The Royal Crematorium has three levels and nine spires to mark the reign of King Bhumibol Adulyadej, also known as Rama IX, the ninth King of the Chakri Dynasty. Its elaborate decorations include a sculpture of Phra Narai, the Hindu god Vishnu, that resembles the face of the late king. At the centre is a 50-metre (164-foot) high funeral pyre with a steel frame covered by intricate wood structures.

Funeral Pyre

Phra Merumas

The "Phra Merumas" funeral pyre symbolizes Mount Meru – a golden mountain in Hindu mythology thought to be the centre of the universe and abode of the gods. A coffin containing the body and a Royal Urn to represent his remains is carried by a gilded chariot to the pyre. The body is cremated using an incinerator set inside the pyre. In the weeks after the funeral, the pyre is taken apart and the wood given to temples and museums.

Monk's Pavilions

Sang

Buddhist monks chant scriptures from these pavilions as the royal remains are placed in the funeral pyre. The monks work in four shifts during the transfer of the body and the cremation.

Dismantling Halls

Ho Plueang

These buildings hold the outer part of the Royal Urn and other items used for the cremation such as wood charcoal, sandalwood flowers and bowls of water.

Heavenly ponds

Sa Anodatrat

Four "heavenly ponds" are set in the bottom corners of the crematorium. The ponds are adorned with sculptures of auspicious animals such as elephants, horses, cows and lions, as well as mythical creatures and angels in the Hindu-Buddhist tradition of the Thai royal court.

Royal Merit-Making Pavilion

Phra Thinang Song Tham

This pavilion hosts merit-making ceremonies attended by Rama X, Thailand's new king, members of the royal family and the Privy Council – the body that advises the king. Foreign heads of state or representatives, diplomats, cabinet members, senior military and civilian officials, members of parliament and high-ranking courtiers also perform merit-making rituals during the ceremony.

Pavilion for Government Officials

Sala Luk Khun

There are six open pavilions inside the ceremonial ground near the Grand Palace where senior government officials are seated here during the funeral ceremony.

Pavilions for Monks, Royal Doctors, and Court Officials

Thim

Pavilion to be used by monks, royal physicians and royal court officials, as well as musicians who play during the ceremony.

Pavilion Describing the Boundary of the Ceremonial Site

Thap Kaset

These buildings are used by government officials attending prayers during the cremation ceremony.

Royal processions

The five-day funeral ceremony officially began on October 25 and includes a number of processions through the streets of Bangkok.

October 26

Remains of the late king are moved in three stages from the Grand Palace to the Royal Crematorium.

The Royal Urn is taken from the Dusit Maha Prasat Throne Hall. It is carried by bearers of the “Phra Yannamas Sam Lam Khan” (Golden Palanquin with Three Poles) in a procession accompanied by 965 troops. The Urn represents the body of the king which is carried separately in a coffin.

The Urn is transferred from the Golden Palanquin to the "Phra Maha Phichai Ratcharot". The Great Victory Chariot, made of wood and decorated with gold and mirrors, dates back to the time of Rama I. Pulled by 216 men, it will carry the ornate urn to the cremation site. The procession includes members of the royal family and involves 2,406 troops.

The Urn is placed on the "Rajarot Puen Yai" (Royal Gun Carriage) and taken around the Royal Crematorium three times in an anti-clockwise direction. It is then carried up a ramp to the funeral pyre for cremation.

The royal family tree

King Bhumibol Adulyadej was the ninth king of the 235-year-old Chakri Dynasty. He ascended to the throne in June 1946 after the still unexplained gunshot death of his elder brother, King Ananda Mahidol. Ananda became Rama VIII after King Prajadhipok abdicated in 1935 following a revolution which ended absolute monarchy in Thailand.

The Chakri Dynasty

The current ruling dynasty dates back to 1782. Following the destruction of Siam’s historic capital in Ayutthaya, the capital was moved south to the town of Thonburi under the leadership of a Thai military commander, later crowned King Taksin. Following his death, the throne fell to General Chakri. As King Yot Fa (Rama I), he founded the present ruling dynasty and moved the capital to Bangkok.
